\begin{abstract}
An ``Authorised Push Payment'' (APP) fraud refers to a case where fraudsters deceive a victim to make payments to bank accounts controlled by them.  The total amount of money stolen via APP frauds is swiftly growing. Although regulators have provided guidelines to improve victims’ protection, the guidelines are vague and the victims are not receiving sufficient protection. To  \emph{facilitate victims' reimbursement}, in this work, we propose a protocol called  ``Payment with Dispute Resolution'' (PwDR) and formally define it. The protocol lets an honest victim prove its innocence to a third-party dispute resolver while preserving the protocol participants' \emph{privacy}. It makes black-box use of a standard online banking system. We evaluate its asymptotic cost and runtime via a prototype implementation. Our evaluation indicates that the protocol is efficient. It imposes only $O(1)$ overheads to the customer and bank.  Moreover, it takes a dispute resolver only $0.09$ milliseconds to settle a dispute between the two parties.
\end{abstract}

%Fair exchange problem captures the case where two mutually distrusted parties want to exchange digital data in a way that neither can cheat its counter-party. The problem’s solutions are often cryptographic schemes, called fair exchange protocols. They have various applications such as the exchange of digital items (e.g., files and digital coins), or the   exchange of digital verifiable  service and digital coins between a buyer and seller. Decades ago, it was  proven that the involvement of  a trusted third-party in the fair exchange protocols is inevitable. The advent of decentralised blockchain offered a possibility to eliminate the trusted party’s involvement. Accordingly, various blockchain-based fair exchange protocols were  proposed. However, as we will show, these solutions are not suitable for the exchange of digital coins and digital verifiable services, e.g., verifiable outsourced data storage, or computation. If they are used naively in such a setting, then serious issues would arise; namely, \emph{real-time information leakage} and \emph{waste of seller's resources}.  In this work, we formally define and propose a generic construction called ``\emph{recurring contingent service payment}'' (RC-S-P) that addresses the aforementioned issues. RC-S-P allows a fair exchange of digital coins and  verifiable service's proof, while ensuring that the buyer  cannot waste the seller's resources, and the parties'   privacy  is preserved.  Although it leverages  smart contracts, the smart contracts-side computation is very low. RC-S-P is the first fair service payment scheme that offers all the above features. Also, we present concrete instantiation of the RC-S-P, when the verifiable service is the ``proofs of retrievability'' (PoR). The instantiation is called “\emph{recurring contingent PoR payment}” (RC-PoR-P). It is primarily based on smart contracts and symmetric-key primitives. We have implemented RC-PoR-P and analysed its cost. Our cost analysis indicates RC-PoR-P is highly efficient. RC-PoR-P is the first PoR scheme that offers all the above appealing features.
